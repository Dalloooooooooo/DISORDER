In a reality where mental health is often treated as an accessory, and where an estimated 4,4% of the global population lives with an Anxiety Disorder (WHO), research on social stigma shows that anxiety disorder is frequently not believed, dismissed as a lie, victimhood, or a mere whim. Anxiety Disorder often remains invisible from the outside, because it tends to exist entirely within the inner space of the individual, hidden, unseen.

This project originates from my Master’s Thesis in Communication Design at Politecnico di Milano and aims to investigate how audiovisual language, and video art in particular, can make perceptible and visible the inner dimension of people living with Anxiety Disorder: a reality that is valid, vast, and profoundly real.

The experimental outcome is based on five interviews with five anonymous individuals living with Anxiety Disorder, structured around the framework of a psychological consultation, and translates their verbally expressed experiences into five video art works.

Developed through an in-depth study of how anxiety disorders have been represented in cinema and of the potential of single-channel video art, the approach is neither strictly documentary nor informational. Instead, it is metaphorical, expressive, and audiovisually experimental.

The language adopted seeks to move beyond linear and rational narration in order to evoke emotional and synesthetic states, engaging the viewer not only in understanding, but above all in feeling:
in approaching what, within those who live with Anxiety Disorder, can be immense, complex, and difficult to imagine from the outside.

I hope this experience stays with you.
